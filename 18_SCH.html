SUBCONJUNCTIVAL

HAEMORRHAGE


Subconjunctival haemorrhage (SCH) presents with a hyperacute and painless (occassional mild foreign body sensation) onset. Dense red well-circumscribed area showing bleeding underneath the conjunctiva in the absence of discharge or inflammation. No change in VA. May be precipitated by coughing/sneezing/vomiting. Risk factors: trauma, contact lens wear, hypertension, diabetes, clotting disorders

Check BP and blood sugar. Establish whether there is a history of clotting problems (can consider FBC). In most cases the patient can be reassured that the blood will self-resolve in 1-2 weeks. It is worth asking whether there was precipitating trauma and establishing that borders of the bleed are visible (this may require lifting eyelids).

Patients are often advised to see their GP for FBC and coagulation screen if the sub-conjunctival haemorrhage has not resolved after 2 weeks.

If the eye is painful or there is associated loss of vision, the clinician should consider retrobulbar haemorrhage, especially in the context of trauma(link). If it was preceipitated by trauma and the clinician is unable to see borders, they should consider globe rupture (link).

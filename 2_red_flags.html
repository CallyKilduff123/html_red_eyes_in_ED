Question: Any red flag features present?

The ophthalmology team would be concerned about endophthalmitis. This is a sight-threatening infection inside the eye and needs to be treated immediately. It can also be caused by injury to the eye or from blebitis from prior glaucoma surgery.

The ophthalmology team would be concerned about an acute pressure rise in the eye. This needs to be treated immediately. Other signs and symptoms can include a fixed mid-dilated pupil, cloudy cornea, reduced vision and haloes when looking at light.

Many conditions cause pain in a red eye, but if the pain is severe, e.g. waking the patient from sleep, it can indicate inflammation in the eye. The ophthalmology team would be concerned about scleritis in particular.

Any acute loss of vision in a red eye requires immediate attention from the ophthalmology team. If you can, try to assess what baseline vision was, the timeline of vision loss and try to measure vision if you have a chart available.

Any trauma to the eye requires a thorough dilated examination by the ophthalmology team. High-velocity, blunt or chemical trauma are particularly concerning. Please note: in chemical injury, the eye must be irrigated throroughly until the pH 7 - follow local or RCEM guidance. Exclude systemic injuries.

Conjunctivitis can cause mucopurulent discharge, beware of profuse purulent discharge i.e. pus reforming soon after wiping, particularly in unilateral cases. Take a sexual history and test for gonorrhoea. Untreated, gonococcal keratoconjunctivitis can rapidly lead to corneal perforation.

In a contact lens wearer, you would be concerned about a corneal infection or ulcer. Ulcers would appear as opacities on the cornea and would stain with fluoroscein under blue light. These would require immediate treatment.

Conditions of concern would include thyroid eye disease, orbital cellulitis, retro-bulbar haematoma or a carotid-cavernous fistula. These all require urgent medical and ophthalmic attention.
